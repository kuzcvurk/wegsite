Michelle McBeth

 
 
 
 
 
This past weekend Sean and I traveled to Charlottesville, VA for their annual book fair. I had a table in the hotel lobby with many other authors and writing related companies. It was quite a learning experience.


The biggest problem is getting people to figure out what you're all about in about a 2 second glance. My book covers don't necessarily inflict "sci-fi" into people's minds, even with the starry background and distinctly tech looking wireframe objects. About 10 minutes after setting up our table, Sean made a small sign that said "sci-fi". It caught the attention of a few people ("Ooh, sic-fi!") but for the most part we still had to try and engage the person as they walked by:


"Hello! Do you like sci-fi?"


"No, sorry." "No, but my grandson does." (Got that one quite often. Apparently Charlottesville is full of older women with techy grandsons.) "I can't even figure out my TV." "Yes!"


Aha! My target audience! All 20 of them!


For the amount of people who actually stopped at my table to talk and find out more about the book, I'd say 30% purchased a book. Of those who didn't, a few took cards to check out my website later, or signed up for my monthly newsletter. A bunch of people had freebies to lure folks in. I'm not a fan of freebies, since I've been to plenty a convention where people just scout tables for free stuff with no intention of engaging any of the vendors. However I do think giving something away with any book purchase is a smart idea. Sean and I were toying with a logo for Lancing Electronics that we could turn into mugs and such. I'm not a fan of taking your book cover and merely slapping it on merchandise. I don't think I've ever seen a book cover that would itself well to that sort of object. The sole exception perhaps being "Hitchhiker's Guide To the Galaxy".


Another idea we had was doing a dramatic interpretation of my book every half hour or so. It would seriously be hilarious.


So to summarize what I learned:


1. Find some way to grab people's attention.

2. Find ways to reach a more targeted audience.

3. Free stuff!


All in all, it was a great experience, and probably a good starter for me.
